Three places where a workflow quietly changed its position.

This page is for recognition, not theory. If one of these looks familiar, the next step is a bounded pilot.

Case 01 - Commercial approval drift

What was expected
The team would confirm scope only after internal approval.
What happened
Outbound wording moved from review language to commitment language before approval was complete.
Where the boundary failed
The workflow did not hold reviewed wording and sent wording to the same approval boundary.
Why it mattered
The company took on a delivery position it had not actually cleared.

Case 02 - Remediation promise drift

What was expected
The response would acknowledge receipt and hold the line until review finished.
What happened
The sent message turned acknowledgment into a promise to resolve and settle.
Where the boundary failed
The workflow allowed a stronger external position than the reviewed internal position.
Why it mattered
A temporary response became an operational commitment with real cost and expectation.

Case 03 - Exception handling drift

What was expected
A special-case request would be held until the named approver signed off.
What happened
The exception moved forward because the workflow treated partial review as if it were final approval.
Where the boundary failed
The workflow could not reliably separate review state from approved state at the point of execution.
Why it mattered
A one-off exception became a real precedent without a clearly authorized decision.
